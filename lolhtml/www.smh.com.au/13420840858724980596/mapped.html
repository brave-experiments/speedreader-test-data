Business leaders call for calm as markets tank despite stimulus

By Sarah Danckert, Charlotte Grieve, Dominic Powell and Colin Kruger
March 12, 2020 — 6.30pm

Business leaders have called for calm after the Australian share market shrugged off the federal government's stimulus package to record its biggest one-day fall since the global financial crisis.

The S&P/ASX200 plunged 7.4 per cent yesterday shedding as much as $120 billion as panic selling reigned amid growing concerns of the impact of COVID-19 on the global economy. The fall took the exchange to 5304.6 points to close at the lowest level since November 2016.

The sharemarket opened the day down around 3 per cent following a weak overnight lead from Wall Street, with the S&P 500 diving 4.9 per cent. The morning dive was initially tempered by the federal government announcing a stimulus package of $17.8 billion aimed at pensioners and small business that was largely well-received by fund managers.

However, panic crept back into the market after US President Donald Trump banned travel from Europe to the US for 30 days, with analysts at stockbroker Bell Potter describing the move as "a free fall".

"The ASX 200 was smashed again as the world seems to have gone into 'lockdown' with Trump banning travel between Europe and the US," the broker said in an afternoon note to clients.

Against this backdrop, former Woolworths chief executive Roger Corbett – who was also sat on the board of the Reserve Bank of Australia for many years including during the GFC – called for cool heads to prevail.

"This is a severe reaction of course but it has to be seen in the overall dimension of the world economy this is clearly a shock, the markets are turning," Mr Corbett told The Age and Sydney Morning Herald.

"Panic is creeping in but, just like in the global financial crisis, the market will recover, it will take some time, but it will recover."

Mr Corbett welcomed the stimulus package that will involve 6 million Australians receiving a one-off payment of $750. "This will help us manage our way through it," he said.

The chief executives of National Australia Bank and BHP issued written statements on Thursday welcoming the stimulus package.

"Banks are well placed to support the Australian community through this unprecedented situation," NAB chief executive Ross McEwan said.

BHP chief Mike Henry welcomed the "calm and methodical approach" being taken by the government.

The boss of one of the country's biggest investment houses, IFM Investors' Brett Himbury, said a lack of information from governments and health authorities was the leading cause of the market panic.

"Even the most seemingly informed authorities, like the WHO, are really unclear as to how big this scenario could get globally," Mr Himbury told The Age and The Sydney Morning Herald.

Another major fund manager, Geoff Wilson of Wilson Asset Management, said it was the swiftest shift to a bear market ever.

"At the moment everyone is driven by fear," he said. "We're in the panic phase."

And while he was happy with the government's fiscal stimulus response, Mr Wilson doesn't think it will be enough to save Australia from a recession. "I think Australia will go into recession because the economy was very patchy before this," he said.

Russel Pillemer, the chief executive of $3 billion fund Pengana Capital and Malcolm Turnbull’s former business partner, said the markets were currently "running on sentiment".

"I think what we saw today was a sort of capitulation we haven’t really seen before. People were selling positions irrespective of valuations, just wanting to get out," he said.

"Most advisers are ringing and reassuring their clients, telling them to not sell now as they’re expecting a bounce back. Most things are below fair value at the moment."

Mr Pillemer said he doesn’t think there should be trading restrictions put on the market at the moment, thinking traders were mostly just "freaked out" and looking to put their money back into cash.

Another investment manager, Atlas Funds Management chief investment officer Hugh Dive, said he could not speculate on how long the coronavirus would continue to rattle markets but the slump was an opportunity for long-term, active investors.

"Coronavirus is not the end of civilisation. People will still have to drive on roads, get pathology tested, need food, need packaging on their food. They'll gamble," Mr Dive said.
