Authorities warn first wave of infections in NSW to top 1.5 million people

By Rachel Clun
March 12, 2020 — 5.37pm

NSW’s Chief Health Officer says authorities are anticipating as many as 1.5 million people in the state to be infected with COVID-19, as hospitals prepare for an outbreak to converge with flu season.

Premier Gladys Berejiklian said events like the Sydney Royal Easter Show would not be cancelled at this stage, but that decision could change.

Most of the 78 cases diagnosed in NSW were in overseas travellers or their close contacts. While many are connected to a community outbreak involving Ryde Hospital, the Dorothy Henderson Lodge aged care facility at Macquarie Park and two nearby schools, the origin of two cases remains unknown.

"For that reason NSW will continue to review all our policies in relation to mass gatherings and where there’s large numbers of people congregating," Ms Berejiklian said.

"To keep you as safe as possible, our advice might change."

The state's Chief Health Officer Kerry Chant said the "best evidence" from modelling of the impact of the disease means health authorities were "anticipating 20 per cent of the first wave [of NSW] to be infected".

While the vast majority of the projected 1.5 million cases will have mild symptoms, some will need hospitalisation.

Dr Chant told a NSW budget estimates hearing on Thursday NSW Health has asked hospitals to double their intensive care capacity in anticipation for a surge of patients.

"Every day our ventilator capacity is filled with people who may need surgery, there’s also a time where we can use and surge into out perioperative areas and turn them into ICUs," she said.

Healthcare workers in hospitals will have restrictions on "non essential leave requests", a NSW Health spokeswoman said, in a bid to manage demand.

During the estimates hearing, NSW Health deputy secretary Phil Minns said no leave was being cancelled at this stage.

"As new leave requests come in they will need to be looked at, approval will be a little bit less automatic than is generally the case," he said.

"There’s still access to leave, there’s no plan to take any more serious measures."

The NSW Health spokeswoman added if staff choose to go to countries where the government's advice is ‘reconsider your need to travel’ or 'exercise a high degree of caution' on the date they leave, which all now require a 14-day isolation period, they would not be granted special leave on their return.

While the advice for mass gatherings was not changing for now, the advice for travellers returning from overseas has been updated.

Dr Chant said they were now asking people returning from any country to avoid large or small gatherings of people including family events for 14 days. She said people should also avoid visiting any hospitals or aged care facilities during that social distancing period.

Health Minister Brad Hazzard said the "big spike in cases" in the space of 24 hours was of a "very big concern" given a large number of people have been testing positive for influenza as well.

Because it was possible for people to contract the flu and COVID-19 at the same time, the minister said he was encouraging everyone to get a flu shot.

"It’s not just the normal target groups that need to get the flu shot, COVID-19 is coming down the line," Mr Hazzard said.

Across Australia, more than 130 people have been diagnosed with coronavirus.

The new cases in NSW include a barista who worked at a Wahroonga cafe, but NSW Health said the person posed "no ongoing risk to people attending," and the risk was low to customers who visited the cafe while the barista was working on several dates between February 19 and March 8.

A 14-year-old girl has also been diagnosed with the disease, however she did not attend school while infectious.
