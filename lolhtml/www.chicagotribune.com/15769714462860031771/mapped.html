The Bulls landed in Florida for a 2-game road trip. A few hours later, the NBA suspended the season.

By Jamal Collier
Chicago Tribune |
Mar 11, 2020 | 10:42 PM

The Bulls were preparing for an adjustment to their season before Tuesday’s game against the Cavaliers as the NBA began taking preventive measures to stop the spread of the coronavirus, but it would have been hard to imagine how the next 24 hours would unfold.

The NBA suspended its season indefinitely after news Wednesday night that a Jazz player tested positive for COVID-19. The league said that player never came to the arena before tipoff in Oklahoma City between the Thunder and Jazz on Wednesday night, but once the positive test was reported, the game was suspended immediately. Several reports said the player who tested positive was All-Star center Rudy Gobert.

Shortly after, the NBA announced it was suspending play indefinitely after Wednesday’s slate of games. It plans to “use this hiatus to determine the next steps for moving forward in regard to the coronavirus pandemic,” the league said in a statement.

The Bulls landed in Orlando, Fla., earlier Wednesday evening, and it was unclear what their travel plans were to get back to Chicago. They were scheduled to play the Magic on Thursday.

Concerns over the coronavirus dominated conversation before Tuesday’s game at the United Center after the league restricted the media’s locker-room access.

Players talked about safety measures they were taking, and a few injured Bulls distributed hand sanitizer on the bench during the game. The new league policy required at least 6 feet between reporters and players, who also considered what it would be like to play in empty arenas.

What they had not considered was getting to this point, especially so quickly.

“It gets scary because it’s unknown,” Zach LaVine said Tuesday. “Obviously if we’re taking precautions like this, it’s getting more and more serious. I just hope everybody is staying safe, staying healthy, staying clean, washing hands and things like that.

“The main thing is it’s unknown for sports and entertainment people, our little circle, and how it could be affected because you’re around so many people, you go on flights all the time, have so many interactions. You see how easy it spreads, so you just want to make sure everyone is safe and doing the right thing. It’s scary being in an unknown situation."
